Maria Ressa in retrospect: The Rappler CEO in focus.

Day after day, page after page in the Philippines’ history, starting from the People Power revolution in 1986, renowned journalist Maria Ressa has been watching from the newsrooms as the Filipino people fought for their democracy. As she traversed through her work in CNN, first as the bureau chief in Manila until 1995 and then as lead investigative reporter in Asia until 2005, all the way to her work as the news division head of ABS-CBN and a writer for the Wall Street Journal, Ressa has observed how progress has been stalled by the repeated mistakes in the institutions that form the foundations of the country.

“How do you strengthen democracy? From my part of the world, waiting for governments to actually strengthen the institutions… while I had faith, the processes of governance are slow, and when you don't have institutions, you repeat the same mistakes over and over,” Ressa said in an interview with Reuters. “I had great faith the technology would actually enable journalists working with our community to push change bottom-up.”

This is what inspired her to co-found Rappler, which has grown to be one of the most well-known, albeit controversial, local online news sites in the country. Building a news site from scratch was no small task, but her vision of reimagining journalism in the Philippines made it a much more daunting feat to achieve. Crowdsourcing was at the very core of Rappler, where social media became a tool to have a direct connection with audiences.

“It's very simple. It's building communities of action, and what builds those communities of actions is journalism, but in the end it's the action part that I was most interested in,” said Ressa. Comparing her work in CNN with Rappler, she reflected on the contrast of having full control over the content they put out and “throwing it into the black box” — television — with no way of knowing the impact on and reactions from the audience immediately, versus the pressure of providing updates in real time without the luxury of time to think everything through, while having an intimate connection to your community through social media.

Maria Ressa
Rappler CEO, Nobel Peace Prize winner

Ressa recalls her experience from when she first started Rappler, where they used to put out traditional 30-minute newscasts but transitioned to 30-second flashes of video after their followers called out to them. “I was laughing because our audience would say, ‘Hey, where are you? We're waiting!’ and I was tweeting back. That's kind of cool (and annoying) but it's so interesting when you put that kind of interactivity and the ability to talk directly to your community.”

As time went by, Rappler’s reach grew, but their style of journalism coupled with fearless reporting put a target on the organization’s back during the Duterte era. Their revelations of corruption by the government and its officials, exposition on the human rights violations committed in the war on drugs, and criticisms on the use of bots and trolls to garner support for the administration through spreading disinformation caused legal problems for Rappler, most notably of which is the revocation of their license as a mass media entity by the Securities Exchange Commission for allegedly violating the Constitution's Foreign Equity Restrictions in Mass Media by being wholly foreign-owned.

Rappler ordered to shut down in 2022 by the Philippine government
Source: International Press Institute

However, Rappler’s unwavering commitment to pursuing truth allows it to stand to this day as a trusted news source for the people. Ressa’s work with the organization was just recently recognized as she, along with Russian journalist Dmitry Muratov, were awarded the Nobel Peace Prize for safeguarding press freedom in the country, specifically with their extensive coverage on the drug war and the spread of fake news in the country.

“Rappler, although it’s a corporation, has been reaching out to so many people over the years and has been so transparent, especially with the kind of work they do on the ground,” said Roma Angelou Dizon, an English teacher from the Philippine Science High School - Central Luzon Campus. “They also [talk about] the things they go through, even if that’s something that is [usually] kept as a corporate secret, but because they’re in journalism and they’re faced with so many state-initiated attacks and malicious commentaries, they make us see their side and also what else is there. They give us the truth, and that’s the most important function of a journalistic institution, especially in the Philippines.”

Ressa's dedication to journalism and Rappler's transparent and courageous reporting resonated deeply with the Filipino people. Despite the challenges and state-initiated attacks, the organization's commitment to delivering the truth has garnered immense support. Rappler's continued efforts to inform and empower the public have reaffirmed the essential role of a journalistic institution in the Philippines.

As Maria Ressa's journey with Rappler continues, her legacy as a champion of democracy and press freedom will inspire future generations of journalists. Through her vision, dedication, and unwavering pursuit of truth, Ressa has proven that journalism can be a catalyst for change and a powerful force in building a stronger, more transparent society.
